### concept: "Tell me about yourself"
The opener, and most people fumble it by reciting their résumé. Use **present → past → future** in ~60–90 seconds: who you are *now* and the scope you operate at (one line), 1–2 **highlight** achievements with impact, then *why you're here* (what you want next, tied to this role). It sets the frame for the whole interview — lead with your strongest, most relevant signal. Don't narrate every job chronologically; curate.

#### probe: How should you structure "tell me about yourself"?
Present–past–future in under ~90 seconds: start with who you are and your current scope ("backend engineer working on multi-tenant systems at scale"), give one or two quantified highlights that matter for *this* role, then pivot to why you're interviewing and what you want next. It's a curated headline, not a chronological résumé read-through — you're choosing the narrative the rest of the interview hangs on. Practice it out loud until it's tight.

### concept: "Why this company / why this role?"
This tests whether you've **done your homework** and have genuine motivation. Connect *their* specifics — the product, the tech stack, the scale of problems, the mission — to *your* goals and strengths. "You run large-scale [X] on [stack I know], and I want to go deeper on [Y]" beats generic flattery. Show you've read about the team/product. Avoid answers that would apply to any company ("good culture, growth").

#### probe: What makes a strong "why us" answer?
Specificity grounded in research: name the actual problems they solve, the scale or tech that excites you, and tie it to where you want to grow — so the answer could *only* be about them, not any company. Show you understand what the team does and why your background fits. Generic answers ("great culture, learning opportunities") signal you didn't prepare; a concrete, two-way fit ("you have X problem, I've done Y and want to do more of it") signals real interest.

### concept: "Why are you leaving / looking?"
Stay **positive and forward-looking** — never trash your current employer (it reads as a red flag and predicts how you'll talk about *them* later). Frame it as moving **toward** something: scope, a kind of problem, growth, technology — not running away. "I've learned a lot, and I'm looking for [bigger scope / new challenge] that this role offers" is the shape. Keep it brief; don't air grievances even if they're real.

#### probe: How do you answer "why are you leaving" without red flags?
Frame it as pull, not push: you're seeking greater scope, a new class of problems, growth, or technology that the new role offers — not escaping a bad situation. Speak respectfully about your current employer regardless of reality, because badmouthing them tells the interviewer how you'd later talk about *this* company. Keep it short and positive, then redirect to what excites you about the opportunity in front of you.

### concept: "Strengths and weaknesses"
For **strengths**, pick one or two **relevant** to the role and back each with brief evidence — don't just claim "problem-solver," show it. For **weaknesses**, give a **real** one and, crucially, what you're **actively doing** about it — that's what's being tested (self-awareness + growth), not the flaw itself. Avoid the fake humblebrag ("I'm a perfectionist / I work too hard") — interviewers see through it. A genuine, owned weakness with a concrete improvement plan lands far better.

#### probe: How do you answer "what's your greatest weakness?"
Name a **real** weakness and pair it immediately with the concrete steps you're taking to improve — the question tests self-awareness and growth mindset, not whether you have flaws. Avoid the transparent humblebrags ("perfectionist," "work too hard"); they signal evasiveness. Pick something genuine but not disqualifying for the role, show you've recognized it and have a system to address it, and ideally note the progress you've already made.

### concept: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"
They're checking **ambition, realism, and fit** — will you grow here and stay a while. Answer with a direction aligned to the role's path (deeper technical mastery, broader scope, mentoring/leading) rather than a rigid title or an unrelated plan. Show you want to **grow with them**. Avoid both extremes: "I have no idea" (no drive) and "I want your job / I'll start my own company" (poor fit). Tie your growth to delivering value for them along the way.

#### probe: What are they really assessing with the 5-year question?
Ambition, self-direction, and retention/fit — whether you have a growth trajectory and whether it aligns with what this role and company can offer. Give a direction (deeper expertise, larger scope, technical leadership/mentoring) consistent with the role's path, framed as growing *with* them while delivering value. Avoid "no idea" (reads as no drive) and anything that signals you'll leave fast or don't fit (a wildly unrelated plan). It's about trajectory, not a precise title.

### concept: Salary, current CTC, and notice period
Handle compensation with preparation, not anxiety. **Research the market range** for the role/level/location beforehand. If pushed early, give a **researched range** ("based on market and my experience, I'm looking at X–Y") or politely defer until you understand the role's scope. Be **honest** about current compensation and **notice period** — don't lie (it surfaces in checks). Negotiate from a **written offer**, not in the screening call, and consider the **total** package (base, bonus, equity, growth), not just base. Stay collaborative — it's not adversarial.

#### probe: How do you handle the salary expectations question?
Come prepared with a researched market range for the role, level, and location. When asked, either give that range with a brief rationale ("based on market and my experience") or defer until you've understood the role's scope, which is reasonable early on. Be honest about current pay and notice period — misrepresenting either risks the offer in verification. Save real negotiation for when you have a written offer, evaluate the total package (not just base), and keep the tone collaborative rather than combative.

### concept: "Do you have any questions for us?"
**Always say yes** — "no questions" signals disinterest. Have 3–4 ready that show seniority and genuine curiosity: the team's biggest current technical challenge, how decisions and disagreements get resolved, what success looks like in the first 6 months, the path from your level to the next. Ask things you actually care about — their answers also help *you* evaluate the role. Avoid questions easily answered by their website, and avoid leading with perks/time-off in early rounds.

#### probe: What questions should you ask the interviewer?
Always have a few — declining signals disinterest and wastes a chance to evaluate them back. Good ones probe the real work and culture: the team's biggest technical challenge right now, how technical decisions and disagreements are made, what success looks like in the first six months, and the growth path from this level. Pick ones you genuinely care about, since the answers inform your decision too. Skip anything answerable from their careers page, and don't open with perks/leave in early rounds.
